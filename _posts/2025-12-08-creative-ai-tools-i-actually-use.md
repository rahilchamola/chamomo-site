Everyone has a list of AI tools they've signed up for. Most of them die in the graveyard of unused browser tabs. Here's what actually stuck in my workflow after six months of testing.

## The daily drivers

**Claude (obviously).** Not just for writing — for thinking. I use it as a sparring partner for ideas, a first-pass editor, and a research accelerator. The key insight: treat it as a colleague who's read everything but experienced nothing. Great for structure, weak on taste.

**Cursor for code.** I'm not a developer by training, but I build tools and prototypes. Cursor's AI pair programming means I can scaffold a Flask app or a React component without drowning in Stack Overflow. It's not replacing engineers — it's making me dangerous enough to prototype.

**Midjourney for visual concepts.** DJ flyers, social media assets, mood boards for set concepts. I don't use the outputs directly (they still look AI-generated), but they're excellent for communicating visual direction to designers or for quick social content.

## The second tier

**Notion AI** for summarizing meeting notes and course readings. Saves about 30 minutes a day during heavy academic weeks.

**Descript** for editing DJ set recordings and podcast-style content. The text-based audio editing is genuinely magical.

## What I stopped using

Everything that required me to change my workflow to fit the tool. If an AI tool asks me to adopt a new process instead of accelerating my existing one, it's dead on arrival. The best tools are invisible — they make what I already do faster.

## The pattern

Tools that work share three traits: low activation energy (one-click or one-prompt to value), composable with existing workflows, and honest about their limitations. The ones that promise to "revolutionize" everything usually do nothing.
