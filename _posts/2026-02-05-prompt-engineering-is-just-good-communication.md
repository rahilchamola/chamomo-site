My strategy professor keeps saying "the quality of your answer depends on the quality of your question." She's talking about case analysis. She's also accidentally describing prompt engineering.

## The parallel is exact

In every MBA class, we learn frameworks for structuring ambiguity. MECE breakdowns. Issue trees. Hypothesis-driven analysis. The whole point is: take a messy problem, decompose it into clear components, and communicate your reasoning path.

That's literally what you do when you write a good prompt.

A bad case presentation: "Tell me about the company's strategy."
A bad prompt: "Write me something about marketing."

A good case presentation: "Given the margin pressure from new entrants, should Company X invest in vertical integration or pursue a platform model? Evaluate based on capability fit, capital requirements, and competitive response timeline."

A good prompt follows the same structure — context, constraint, evaluation criteria, desired format.

## What MBA teaches you about prompts

**Specificity reduces variance.** In a case discussion, vague questions get vague answers. Same with LLMs. The more precise your framing, the tighter the output distribution.

**Structure is a gift to the reader.** Consultants use pyramid principles because executives have no time. LLMs respond better to structured inputs for the same reason — clear hierarchy helps the model allocate attention.

**Iteration beats perfection.** You don't solve a case in one pass. You build a hypothesis, test it against data, refine. Prompting works the same way. The first output is a draft. The conversation is the analysis.

## The meta-skill

The real takeaway: prompt engineering isn't a technical skill. It's a **thinking** skill dressed up in a technical costume. People who communicate well — who can decompose problems, specify constraints, and iterate on feedback — are already good at prompting.

The MBA students who struggle with AI tools are usually the same ones who struggle to frame a case clearly. Fix the thinking, the prompting follows.
