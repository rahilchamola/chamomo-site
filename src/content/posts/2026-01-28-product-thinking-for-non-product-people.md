I keep telling my MBA classmates that I "think in product." Most of them think this means I want to be a PM. It doesn't. It means I evaluate everything through a specific lens.

## The product lens

Every system has users, jobs-to-be-done, friction points, and feedback loops. A DJ set has these. An MBA study group has these. A personal website has these.

Product thinking is the practice of making these elements explicit, then optimizing for the ones that matter most.

## Applied examples

**A DJ set is a product.** The user is the crowd. The job-to-be-done is emotional transportation — they want to feel something they can't feel alone. Friction points are bad transitions, energy dips, and genre whiplash. The feedback loop is real-time: are they dancing or checking their phones?

**An MBA study system is a product.** The user is future-me during exams. The job-to-be-done is efficient recall under time pressure. Friction points are scattered notes, missing context, and low-quality summaries. The feedback loop is exam performance.

**This website is a product.** The user is anyone curious about what I'm building. The job-to-be-done is understanding my thinking and deciding if they want to collaborate, hire, or follow along. Friction is slow loads, confusing navigation, and content that doesn't match expectations.

## The framework

For any system, ask:

1. **Who is the user?** (Be specific — "everyone" is nobody)
2. **What job are they hiring this for?** (Clayton Christensen's JTBD framework)
3. **Where is the friction?** (Map the experience, find the drops)
4. **What's the feedback mechanism?** (How do you know if it's working?)
5. **What's the one metric that matters?** (If you could only measure one thing)

This works for products, services, events, personal systems, and career decisions. It's an operating system, not a job function.
