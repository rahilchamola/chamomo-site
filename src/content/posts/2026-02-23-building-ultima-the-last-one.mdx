Ultima. Latin. It means last. Final. Terminal point in a sequence. I chose it deliberately. Not as marketing. As a constraint. Every time I feel the urge to add an integration, every moment I'm tempted to just hook into Slack or Zapier or whatever the new hotness is, the name stops me cold. Is this *ultima*, or is it just another middleware layer pretending to be a product?

The productivity app graveyard lives in a folder on my Mac called "Apps (not using)." Todoist. Habitica. Bear. Day One. Fantastical. Things 3. Notion. Obsidian. Each one is meticulously crafted. Each one is better than the last at doing exactly one thing. I paid for most of them. Used each between two weeks and three months. Stopped using all of them.

Not because they're bad. They're objectively good at what they do. The problem is structural. They're good at exactly one thing and assume everything else is someone else's problem.

## The federation trap

The industry's answer to this has always been integrations. Connect everything. Zapier as the nervous system. IFTTT as the glue. Your life federated across eight apps, each with a partial picture, each sending data through webhooks to try and make the others aware of what just happened.

It sounds reasonable. It feels like abundance.

It is not abundance. It is fragmentation pretending to be connection.

When I look at my own productivity stack over the past three years, I can trace the problem precisely. Todoist doesn't know I journaled about feeling depleted. Bear doesn't see that I skipped my habits. Habitica has no idea I have a 90-minute gap at 2pm. Things 3 doesn't know I have therapy at 4pm that will drain me. No single app sees the human. They see the task. The habit. The memory. The calendar slot. But not the person inside all of it.

So I end up making worse decisions. I assign myself a goal in one app. I don't complete it because I'm exhausted from events tracked in another app. I don't get feedback that I'm exhausted because my journal is siloed. The federation model means every tool is blind to 80% of what's actually happening.

## ADHD and executive function

ADHD makes this problem structurally catastrophic.

For most people, the federation model is annoying. For ADHD brains, it's a permission slip to fail.

Executive function is a finite resource. Every context switch costs. Every decision point drains the same limited well. Every app boundary is a decision gate. Am I adding this to Todoist or Notion? Should I log this in Day One or in the habit tracker? The overhead of choosing *where* to record something becomes so expensive that the recording never happens.

Then the apps, each blind and loyal to their own domain, keep sending conflicting instructions. Habitica says "you're on a 47-day streak, don't break it." Things 3 says "you have 23 overdue tasks." Todoist sends a notification about a task due three months ago that I actually deleted. Notion reminds me I haven't updated my goal tracker. They're all right according to their own models. They're all completely useless because they're all describing a different game.

What I needed wasn't better apps. What I needed was no apps. One app. One database. One unified model of my cognitive state.

## The Ultima thesis

One system with complete context beats ten systems with perfect features.

That's the bet.

Ultima isn't five apps trying to hold hands. It's one app with four native engines that all read from the same database and write to the same state.

A todo engine. A habits engine. A journal engine. A calendar lens (reading your Google Calendar, not replacing it—that's already perfect). Not federated. Integrated at the database level. When the LLM asks "what should I do right now?" it's reading a unified model.

Journaled about feeling depleted? The context assembler sees it.
Skipped habits? It knows.
Have a 90-minute gap at 2pm? It can see the slot.
Therapy at 4pm? It knows about the drain.

The system has the full picture. For the first time, the recommendation is made with complete context.

## The stack, briefly

I'm building it in Next.js. Backend is Supabase—one database for all four modules. A context assembler that reads the current state across all four domains and packages it into a structured prompt. Claude Haiku for speed, Claude Sonnet for synthesis tasks. The system asks one question per day: "Given everything you know about this person right now—their energy, their commitments, their goals, their recent mindset—what should they do in this moment?"

That question is the entire product. Everything else is infrastructure.

## Naming as philosophy

I studied philosophy at ISB before the MBA. Naming matters more than most people think. Most productivity apps name themselves after mechanisms. Things. Todoist. Notion. They describe the tool.

I wanted a name describing the intention. What is this tool for in the arc of a life? What role is it supposed to play?

Ultima says: stop optimizing your stack. Stop adding tools. Stop trying to make the federation work. This is the last one. Design it to be unified at the foundation. Accept nothing less.

The name is a philosophy. Every time I'm tempted to add a Slack integration or a calendar sync or a third-party vendor, the name asks: is this making the system more unified or more federated? Does it give the LLM more context or just more noise?

If the answer is "more federated," it doesn't ship.

## One system versus eight systems

This isn't about feature parity. Todoist will always be better at pure task management than Ultima. Habitica will always have better streak mechanics. Day One will always have more beautiful journal templates. Fantastical will always have a more powerful calendar.

That's not the point. The point is that when you're tired and confused and you open your phone at 8am, you don't need the best task manager in the world. You need to know what to do. Given your energy. Given your obligations. Given what you've been thinking about lately. Given what you actually skipped yesterday.

Ultima is betting that one system with complete context will give better recommendations than eight systems with perfect features but mutual blindness.

The productivity app graveyard can stay full. Ultima is designed to be the last thing you install, not another layer in the tower.
