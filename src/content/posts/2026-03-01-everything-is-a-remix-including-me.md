I'm a DJ who thinks about product strategy. An MBA student who builds websites. A technologist who cares about vibe. None of these identities make sense in isolation. Together, they're a remix.

## The remix principle

Kirby Ferguson nailed it: everything is a remix. Copy, transform, combine. But most people stop at copying. The magic is in the **transform** step — taking something from one context and applying it in a completely different one.

When I apply DJ set structure to blog post pacing, that's a remix. When I use MBA frameworks to analyze my DJ content strategy, that's a remix. When I build personal productivity systems using product management principles, that's a remix.

## Why remix beats originality

"Original" ideas are usually just remixes with the sources hidden. The most creative people I know are prolific consumers across domains. They read widely, experience diversely, and constantly ask: "what would this look like in a different context?"

The advantage of being explicit about remixing is that it gives you a **method**. Instead of waiting for inspiration, you can systematically:

1. Collect interesting ideas from diverse domains
2. Identify the underlying principle (not the surface form)
3. Apply that principle to your current problem
4. Test whether the transfer works

## The personal brand implication

"Remix artist across domains" isn't just a tagline. It's a positioning strategy. In a world where everyone is specializing, being a skilled cross-pollinator is increasingly rare and valuable.

The MBA student who can explain product strategy through music metaphors. The DJ who can analyze crowd dynamics using data frameworks. The builder who understands that technical systems and human experiences follow the same design principles.

That's the remix. That's the brand.
