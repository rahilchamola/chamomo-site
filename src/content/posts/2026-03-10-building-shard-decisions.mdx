The best product decisions in Shard weren't what I built. They were what I killed.

AI makes building cheap. You can scaffold a feature in an afternoon. That same cheapness applies to killing. If rebuilding costs an hour, removing a broken feature isn't a loss. It's a trade: one hour of rebuild versus infinite hours debugging something that almost works.

"Almost works" is the most dangerous state in software. It passes your tests. It demos well. Then it fails silently in production. 90% reliable means 1 in 10 players hits a wall. That's not a feature. That's a bug with good marketing.

## The deeper stories

### SSE streaming

Token-by-token streaming was beautiful. You could watch the AI's words appear one at a time, like the game was thinking out loud. It felt alive.

It hung on turn 2. The first turn streamed perfectly. The second turn, with a larger context window, would stall mid-sentence. I spent an hour trying to fix it. Timeouts, retry logic, chunked fallbacks. Every fix introduced a new edge case. I killed it.

The replacement: a blocking generate call with a client-side typewriter animation. The player sees the same character-by-character reveal. The underlying mechanism is completely different, and completely reliable.

### D&D-default genres

The problem was structural. The engine's world-generation pipeline had been trained (through my own prompt examples) on fantasy tropes. I rewrote the entire genre system in a day. Seven archetype lists for fundamentally different settings. Genre-specific vocabulary banks, character name pools, location templates. The pipeline became genre-agnostic.

### Sparse choice gating

I thought players would love freetext freedom. Playtests showed the opposite. When given choices, players were bolder. They picked the risky option. They explored edges. Choices gave them permission to be dramatic.

I switched to an always-decision policy. Every turn presents choices. Players can still override with freetext, but the default is curated options. The product insight: like DJing, you're not playing open mic. You're reading the crowd and picking from a curated crate. The constraint is the creative space.

## What survived

Not everything got killed. The features that survived share a trait: they work 100% of the time, or they degrade gracefully.

The effect bus always processes effects, even if the AI generates an unexpected one (the validator catches and patches it). The coherence stack always checks facts, even if it occasionally flags something that wasn't actually a contradiction (false positives are acceptable, false negatives aren't). The relationship model always updates, even if the magnitude of change is debatable.

The survivors are systems. The kills were features. Systems are durable because they define how things work. Features are fragile because they define what things look like.

**The vibe-coding takeaway: kill features fast. AI makes rebuilding cheap, so killing is cheap too. Ship what always works.**
