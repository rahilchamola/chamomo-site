## The deeper cuts

### 1. Design the game, not the AI

33 TypeScript interfaces before one LLM call. Every relationship axis, effect type, and world structure existed as a type definition before any AI generated a single word.

The temptation is to start prompting and see what happens. Resist it. TypeScript interfaces are game design documents that happen to be executable. Use them that way.

### 2. Separate generation from governance

The AI generates. Pure functions govern. This is non-negotiable. In Shard, the AI proposes what happens next. TypeScript functions validate those proposals against game state, apply corrections, and execute the state mutations.

Every bug I had in the first month was a case where I let the AI skip the governance layer. "It's a simple update, I'll let the model handle it directly." No. Every shortcut becomes a debugging nightmare at scale.

### 3. 90% reliable = broken

SSE streaming worked perfectly on the first turn. Hung on the second. The parser worked on clean outputs. Choked on edge cases. The NPC portrait generator produced beautiful images. Different faces for the same character.

Kill the 90% feature. Replace it with a 100% feature that's less impressive.

### 4. Playtests reveal everything

The Anika session. Feb 19. One friend playing the game for 30 minutes produced more useful feedback than a week of solo testing. The entire v0.9.5 roadmap came from that one session.

Vibe-coding makes building fast. That speed is wasted if you're building the wrong thing.

### 5. Match model to task

Structured data needs a fast, precise model. Narrative prose needs a creative, expressive model. Using the same model for everything is like using a chef's knife to eat soup.

### 6. Prompts are teaching documents

My prompts included example outputs with certain formatting. The AI learned from those examples that the formatting was optional. It wasn't. The formatting was structural, and deviations broke the parser.

Write prompts like you're training a smart but literal student. Every example teaches a pattern.

### 7. The AI doesn't make product calls

Architecture, UX patterns, what to ship, what to kill. These are human judgment calls that AI can inform but shouldn't make. Vibe-coding is a PM building a product, not an engineer writing software.

### 8. Type safety catches what AI misses

Day 6. I asked AI coding agents to generate 5 world definition files. They contained 50+ type errors. TypeScript's compiler caught every error before it reached production. Use strict mode. Trust the red squiggles.

### 9. Emergent beats prescribed

No fixed skill trees. No hardcoded item lists. The AI invents all of it contextually. In a pirate world, skills emerge around sailing and swordfighting. In a cyberpunk world, skills emerge around hacking and implants. Less code, more emergent behavior.

### 10. Speed is a thesis, not a shortcut

Shard took one person a few weeks to build. That's not a flex. It's a thesis. Modern AI tools let a solo product person ship what would have taken a team months.

But speed without judgment is just fast failure. The 12 design sessions before the sprint weren't slow. They were the investment that made the sprint productive.

Vibe-coding is fast. The decisions about what to build, what to kill, and what to ship are what make that speed valuable. Like DJing. You can beatmatch any two tracks in seconds. Reading the crowd to know which track to play next, that's the skill.

**The vibe-coding takeaway: speed is the tool. Judgment is the craft. The AI handles velocity. You handle direction.**
