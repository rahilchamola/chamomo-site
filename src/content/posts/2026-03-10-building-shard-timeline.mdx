The story isn't "I built a game in 8 days." The story is: I spent weeks designing a game engine with AI partners, then sprinted for 8 days to ship it.

Two acts. The design sessions that built the architecture. The sprint that turned architecture into a product.

## Act 1: The design sessions

Before the first git commit, Shard went through 12 full design sessions. These weren't coding marathons. They were product design sprints where I was making decisions about game systems, data models, and player experience with AI coding partners translating those decisions into architecture.

**Session 1 (Pre-v0.1).** The vision crystallized. The core bet: the LLM is the engine, not the game. I picked the tech stack, defined the design philosophy, and wrote the first TypeScript interfaces.

**Session 2 (v0.2).** The runtime engine came together. Effect applicator, narrative director, coherence checker, condition evaluator. Four systems that would become the spine of everything.

**Session 3 (v0.3).** The relationship model. I rejected simple like/dislike scales and built a 6-axis system: trust, fear, desire, respect, rivalry, debt.

**Session 4 (v0.4).** Living character sheets. The player's profile is inferred from their actions, not declared in a character creation screen.

**Session 5 (v0.5).** World Builder platform and the consequence system. I was playing BitLife at the time and noticed how satisfying it is when small choices cascade into big outcomes.

**Sessions 6-8 (v0.6 to v0.8.x).** UI overhaul after UI overhaul. Settings, home screen, choice and freetext redesign. RPG systems: AI-driven items, skills, and quests with no hardcoded trees.

**Session 9 (v0.8.4).** The first formal world, Archons & Ashes. Player name modal. RuneScape-inspired choice pills.

**Session 10 (v0.8.6).** The big reliability breakthrough. A 5-stage approach to parsing AI responses that finally made outputs consistent enough to trust.

**Session 11 (v0.8.8).** The dual-LLM architecture split. One model decides WHAT happens. Another writes HOW it's described. This single decision fixed more bugs than any refactor before or after.

**Session 12 (v0.9.0).** Player identity system and the genre-inclusive engine rewrite. Every world I generated kept defaulting to medieval fantasy. I killed the D&D bias and rebuilt the generation pipeline to be genre-agnostic.

By the end of Session 12, the codebase was at 83,696 lines across 213 files. Tagged v0.9.0. Ready to sprint.

## Epilogue: v0.14

The sprint ended on March 10. On March 11, I added two more major systems.

The Identity Engine (689 lines). Tracks not just what the player does, but who they're becoming. Are they a negotiator or a fighter? A loner or a leader? The game adapts its offerings based on emerging identity, not declared class.

The Consequence Engine (234 lines). Formalizes how effects cascade. A single choice can ripple through relationships, quest states, world events, and NPC behaviors.

21 worlds audited and retrofitted with the new systems. 54+ effect types. 14 versions from v0.1 to v0.14-alpha.

## The numbers

12 design sessions before the first commit. 8 days of sprinting. 125 git commits. 134,000+ lines of TypeScript. 21 worlds. 54+ effect types. 8 director personas. 6-axis relationship model. 5 coherence layers. 1 builder.

**The vibe-coding takeaway: progressive complexity. Start simple. Add layers when the product demands it, not when the architecture permits it.**
