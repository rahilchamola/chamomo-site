## The insight that changed everything

Most AI applications treat the model as an oracle. You send a prompt. You get text back. You display it.

Shard doesn't work that way. The AI isn't answering questions — it's *executing a simulation*. Every player turn triggers a pipeline where specialized models handle different cognitive tasks: one reasons about what changes in the world, another decides what the player should experience, a third writes the prose.

This isn't a design preference. It's the only architecture that produces coherent long-form interactive fiction. And the entire thing was designed in the first two days of the sprint.

## Three layers

Shard's architecture is three layers deep. Each layer has a different rate of change and a different relationship to the AI.

<ArchitectureDiagram client:visible />

**Layer 1: World Definition** is static. TypeScript modules that define everything about a game world — lore, geography, characters, story arcs, rules. Version-controlled, shareable, completely deterministic. No AI touches this layer at runtime. It's the ground truth.

**Layer 2: Cast & Scenario** is dynamic but structured. Character states, relationship values, quest progress — all changing every turn, but through typed effects. When the AI says "the blacksmith now trusts you more," that's a `RelationshipEffect` processed through a mutation engine, not free-text appended to a prompt.

**Layer 3: Runtime Engine** is where AI lives. The multi-step pipeline processes player input, simulates consequences, and generates narrative output. But it never touches state directly — it proposes effects that the engine validates and applies.

This separation is the entire trick. The AI operates on structured state. It doesn't *own* state.

## The multi-model pipeline

The single biggest architectural decision happened on Day 2 — March 4. I moved from one AI call per turn to three specialized calls in sequence.

**Step 1 — Simulation** (fast model, structured JSON): "Given this world state and this player action, what changes? Which characters react? What consequences propagate?" The output is a typed set of effects, not prose.

**Step 2 — Direction** (medium model, scene planning): "Given these simulated changes, what should the player experience? What's the emotional beat? What choices should be available?" This is the cinematic layer — pacing, framing, dramatic structure.

**Step 3 — Narration** (quality model, literary prose): "Write this scene." The narration model gets the simulation output and the directorial notes, then produces the actual text the player reads.

The result: simulation accuracy went up (specialized models are better at structured reasoning), narrative quality went up (the writing model only writes, it doesn't also have to simulate), and coherence across turns went from "frequently broken" to "reliably maintained."

The tradeoff was real. Three API calls per turn instead of one. Higher latency. More complex error handling. But the insight held: **cost-per-turn is the wrong metric. Cost-per-wow-moment is the right one.**

17 commits on Day 2 alone. The pipeline was the pivot point.

## The provider-agnostic bet

From Day 1, I built an abstraction layer over the AI providers. A unified interface that any provider implements.

This felt like over-engineering in a sprint. I was using one provider. Why abstract?

Because on Day 2, mid-afternoon, I needed to swap providers. Model quality was wrong for the simulation step. The swap was a config change, not a rewrite. Zero game logic touched. Over the 8-day sprint, I swapped primary providers three times.

The lesson generalizes: **any dependency that changes faster than your product ships needs an abstraction layer.** When you're vibe-coding at sprint speed, dependencies change fast.

## The coherence problem

Here's the thing nobody talks about in AI product development: coherence over time.

Getting an AI to produce good output on turn 1 is easy. Good prompting, structured input, quality model. Done.

Getting it to stay coherent at turn 50 — remembering that the player betrayed the merchant in act one, that the guard captain's trust has been slowly eroding, that the quest objective changed when the player made an unexpected choice — that's an architecture problem, not a model problem.

Shard uses a 5-layer coherence system, finalized around Day 7. Each layer catches different kinds of drift: factual consistency, relationship continuity, narrative arc progression, world rule compliance, and tonal consistency across director personas. The output of each validation layer feeds into the next turn's context.

This is the moat. Not which model you use. Not how clever your prompts are. Whether your architecture can maintain coherent state across hundreds of interactions.

## System depth at v0.12.3

Here's what the system looks like after 8 days of compressed development:

<SystemComplexity client:visible systems={metricsData.systemComplexity} />

6 relationship dimensions per character pair. 12 game overlays that activate based on narrative context. 8 visual themes that adapt to genre. 30 distinct message types — from standard narration to intercepted radio transmissions. 12 director personas that shape the AI's narrative voice.

None of this was planned upfront. It emerged from the type system. Define an interface, implement the behavior, see what the game can now express. The type system made this kind of rapid depth-building safe — every new system was constrained by its types, and the compiler caught integration issues immediately.

At vibe-coding speed, that safety net isn't optional. It's the reason you can move this fast without the whole thing collapsing.

## The real lesson

AI as a runtime isn't just an architecture pattern. It's a product philosophy.

When AI is a feature, the product exists independently and AI makes it better. When AI is the runtime, the product *can't exist* without AI — but it can do things no non-AI product could ever do.

Shard can generate experiences that no human wrote. Not because the AI is creative (it's not, in any meaningful sense) — but because the *system* is creative. Typed state plus simulation plus direction plus narration, all running on structured data, produces emergent narrative that surprises even me.

That's what "AI as game engine" actually means. And with the right tools and the right architectural instincts, you can design the whole thing in two days.
