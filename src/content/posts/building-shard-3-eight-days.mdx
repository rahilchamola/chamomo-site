## Eight days is a lot longer than you think

When I say "8 days," people hear "hackathon." Something slapped together over a weekend with energy drinks and prayer.

That's not what happened. Eight days of compressed development — vibe-coding with AI as a full development partner — is more like eight weeks of traditional solo work. Every hour produced decisions that would normally take a day. The feedback loop between thinking and shipping collapsed to minutes.

A few things jump out:

**Lines of code grew fastest on Days 5–6.** The first two days were architecture — types, state engine, pipeline design. Fewer lines, higher leverage. Once the foundation was solid, the code poured. Day 6 alone: 10,000 new lines and 33 commits.

**Worlds lagged deliberately.** I had one world until Day 3. One. Not because I couldn't build more, but because one world with full system depth taught more than a dozen shallow ones would have. When worlds 2–6 came on Day 3, the tooling was already in place. Worlds 7–10 landed on Day 6 in a single session.

**Effect types and message types are the real complexity indicators.** Lines of code is vanity. Effect types — the number of distinct things the AI can do to the world — and message types — the number of distinct ways the game can present information — are what determine expressive capability. Both grew steadily, then exploded on Day 6 when the UX layer came together.

## Day by day

### Day 1 — Foundation (Mar 3) · 6 commits

Defined 33+ TypeScript interfaces. Built the state mutation engine. Created the first world. First full 20-turn playthrough where characters maintained consistent behavior.

The commit log reads: **"v0.1 — It works. The characters remember."**

5,000 lines. The entire type system — relationships, quests, effects, locations — laid down in a single day. This felt slow in a sprint. It was the most important day of all eight.

### Day 2 — Pipeline Revolution (Mar 4) · 17 commits

The architectural pivot. Moved from one AI call per turn to three: simulate, direct, narrate. Each step handled by a specialized model optimized for its task.

Mid-afternoon, the simulation model wasn't performing. Swapped providers. Config change, not a rewrite. The provider-agnostic abstraction I'd built on Day 1 — which felt like over-engineering at the time — paid for itself in twenty minutes.

17 commits. 7,000 new lines. The pipeline was the single highest-impact change of the entire sprint.

### Day 3 — Reliability (Mar 5) · 5 commits

Hardening day. Built a 5-stage response parser. Added coherence validation. Killed token-by-token streaming after it silently hung on multi-turn sessions — a feature that worked 90% of the time, which meant it was broken.

Jumped from 1 world to 6. The tooling I'd built while going deep on world 1 made the next five dramatically cheaper to produce.

Only 5 commits, but each one was surgical. The system went from "it works if you're lucky" to "it works."

### Day 4 — Systems Integration (Mar 6) · 8 commits

Shipped all 12 game overlays in one day: battle, cards, romance, interrogation, infiltration, negotiation, shop, crafting, evidence board, rivalry, temptation, duel of wits. Each one is a typed system that the AI can invoke contextually — not a separate screen, but a layer that activates within the narrative flow.

Added PWA support. The game now runs offline-first.

The type system made this possible. Each overlay implements a typed interface. Define the shape, wire up the behavior, the compiler catches every integration issue. At vibe-coding speed, that safety net is the difference between shipping 12 systems in a day and shipping zero.

### Day 5 — Living World (Mar 7) · 15 commits

The wow moment. NPCs started acting autonomously — doing things when the player wasn't interacting with them. World events firing independently. Locations developing their own rhythms.

First playtester said: *"The world feels alive even when I'm not doing anything."*

That sentence is the product. Everything else is infrastructure to make that sentence true.

### Day 6 — Polish Sprint (Mar 8) · 33 commits

The biggest day. 33 commits. 10,000 new lines.

The UX transformation: 20+ message types (from standard narration to intercepted radio transmissions). Adaptive overlays. 8 visual themes that shift with genre. 12 director personas — AI narrative personalities that shape pacing and prose. Skill discovery cards. Inline game HUDs. Scaled from 6 worlds to 10.

Submitted the full architecture to two independent auditors for review. Not friends. Not collaborators. People whose job it was to find problems. The output: more actionable improvements than the previous two days of solo development combined.

**Same AI output. Different presentation. Completely different experience.** That's the lesson of Day 6: UX is half the product.

### Day 7 — Auth & Scale (Mar 9) · 32 commits

Authentication system. Multi-user support. Achievement tracking. LLM observability dashboard. World builder with AI-assisted generation. The 5-layer coherence system finalized — factual consistency, relationship continuity, narrative arc progression, world rule compliance, tonal consistency.

32 commits. The system went from "works for me" to "works for anyone."

### Day 8 — Ship It (Mar 10) · 9 commits

Final hardening. Edge case fixes. Deployment prep. 9 quiet commits after seven days of intensity.

The codebase: 50,000 lines. 150 files. 10 worlds. 40 effect types. 30 message types. 125 total commits across 8 days.

**Shipping is a decision, not a state.** The product isn't the code. The product is what happens when someone else uses it.

## What the timeline doesn't show

It doesn't show the 2 AM decision on Day 2 to scrap an approach that was almost working and redesign the pipeline from scratch. It doesn't show the moment on Day 5 when the NPCs started behaving in ways I hadn't explicitly programmed and I just sat there watching. It doesn't show the emotional difficulty of getting external critique on Day 6 — having someone calmly point out blind spots you'd rationalized into architecture.

Eight days of building at this intensity means living inside the system. You develop instincts about where the load-bearing walls are. You know which abstractions will hold and which ones are about to crack.

Vibe-coding at sprint speed isn't about going fast. It's about compressing the feedback loop between intention and implementation so tightly that every decision gets tested in minutes, not months.

50,000 lines. 8 days. 125 commits. Those are the numbers. The story is compressed, intense, and more interesting than any timeline can capture.
