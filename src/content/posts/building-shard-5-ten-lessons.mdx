## The five AI lessons, expanded

### 1. AI is a runtime, not a feature

This is the foundational insight. The interesting AI products aren't "apps with AI bolted on." They're products where AI is the execution layer — where the thing literally cannot exist without AI, and where that dependency creates capabilities impossible in traditional software.

Shard isn't a text adventure with an AI chatbot. It's a simulation engine where AI processes typed state, proposes effects, and generates narrative — all within an architecture designed from the ground up for AI as the runtime.

**Generalizability: High.** This framing applies to any AI product. Ask: "is AI making an existing thing better, or enabling an entirely new thing?"

### 2. Structured output beats free text

When the AI returns typed JSON — a set of `RelationshipEffect` and `QuestProgressEffect` objects — you can build systems on it. Validate it. Compose it. Test it.

When the AI returns free text, you're parsing hope.

The entire state mutation engine in Shard depends on structured AI output. Every turn, the simulation model returns a JSON object conforming to a TypeScript interface. If it doesn't parse, the turn retries. If it parses but fails validation, the effects get filtered. The system is robust because the contract is explicit.

**Generalizability: Very high.** If you're building anything on AI, invest in structured output pipelines. The upfront cost is dwarfed by the debugging time you save.

### 3. Multi-model pipelines are underrated

The industry discourse is dominated by "which model is best?" Wrong question. The right question: "which models, in what sequence, for what tasks?"

Shard uses fast models for simulation (structured reasoning over state), medium models for direction (scene planning), and quality models for narration (literary prose). Total cost per turn is higher than a single call. Quality is incomparably better. I discovered this on Day 2 and it became the architectural pivot of the entire project.

**Generalizability: Medium.** Multi-model pipelines add complexity. They're worth it when your product has distinct cognitive tasks with different quality/speed tradeoffs. For simple Q&A or search, a single model is fine.

### 4. Coherence is the moat

Can you stay coherent at turn 50? At turn 100? Can the AI remember that the player betrayed the merchant, that the guard captain's trust has been slowly eroding, that the quest objective changed three acts ago?

This isn't a model problem. No model "remembers" by default. Coherence requires architecture: layered validation, structured state management, context assembly that prioritizes relevant history.

Shard's 5-layer coherence system — finalized on Day 7 — is the hardest piece of engineering in the whole project. It's also the one that competitors can't replicate by switching to a better model.

**Generalizability: High for any long-running AI interaction.** If your product involves more than a few turns of context, coherence architecture matters more than model selection.

### 5. Type systems are design tools

This one's sneaky. TypeScript interfaces aren't just engineering artifacts — they're design documents that the compiler enforces.

"A relationship has six dimensions: trust, fear, desire, respect, rivalry, debt." That's a type definition. It's also a game design decision. Change the type, the compiler tells you every system that assumed the old shape. You can't accidentally ship inconsistent game mechanics because the types won't let you.

At vibe-coding speed — shipping 12 new systems in a single day — that safety net is the difference between controlled expansion and structural collapse.

**Generalizability: High for complex products.** Less relevant for simple applications. But any product with interacting systems benefits from treating types as design constraints.

## The five product lessons, expanded

### 6. Depth beats breadth at every stage

One world with rich systems beats ten shallow ones. This was counterintuitive and countermarket. Every competitor was shipping content volume. I shipped system depth.

The result: the first world produced emergent interactions that became the product's differentiator. And the tooling I built to create that depth made subsequent worlds dramatically cheaper to produce — worlds 2–6 came on Day 3 in a fraction of the time world 1 took.

**Generalizability: Depends on your market.** Content-driven products need volume eventually. But building depth first creates the machinery for efficient breadth later.

### 7. First architecture is the most important product decision

Features can be added, changed, and removed. Architecture can't — not without a rewrite. The type system, the state engine, the separation of simulation from presentation — those decisions from Day 1 are still the load-bearing walls at Day 8 and beyond.

I got lucky. The early architecture decisions were good enough to survive 10x growth in a week. But it wasn't really luck — it was spending the first day on types before writing game logic.

**Generalizability: Universal.** Spend disproportionate time on architecture decisions. They compound.

### 8. External critique is the highest-ROI activity

Two independent reviewers, a few hours each, on Day 6. The output: more actionable improvements than the previous two days of solo development. Blind spots I'd rationalized. Complexity I'd normalized. Assumptions I'd mistaken for constraints.

I almost skipped it. The sprint momentum made stopping feel expensive. It was the cheapest investment of the entire build.

**Generalizability: Universal.** If you're building solo, schedule external critique before you think you need it. Mid-sprint, not after.

### 9. UX is half the product

Same AI output, presented through 3 message types with basic formatting: feels like a chatbot. Same AI output, presented through 30 message types with adaptive overlays, visual themes, and director personas: feels like a cinematic experience.

The AI didn't get better. The *experience* did. And the experience is what users pay for. The Day 6 UX overhaul — 33 commits — transformed Shard from a tech demo into a product.

**Generalizability: Universal.** Especially for AI products, where the raw output is often impressive but the presentation is often terrible.

### 10. Kill features that work 90% of the time

Token-by-token streaming worked 90% of the time. It silently hung the other 10%. A feature that fails every ten uses is a broken product.

This extends beyond reliability. Features that work "mostly" create the most insidious kind of technical debt — they're too functional to remove but too unreliable to trust. Remove them. Replace them with something that works 100% of the time, even if it's less ambitious.

In a sprint, the cut is easy — you barely have time to get attached. The lesson transfers to any timescale: **90% reliable is 100% unshippable.**

## The lesson I'm still learning

Eight days in, 50,000 lines deep, and the biggest thing I still struggle with: knowing when to stop building and start shipping.

The builder instinct says there's always one more system to add, one more edge case to handle, one more layer of polish. The product instinct says ship it, learn from real users, iterate.

Shard is close. The architecture is solid. The experience is good. The coherence holds. But "close" is a dangerous word for a solo builder who finds the building more interesting than the launching.

If you're reading this and recognizing yourself — the person who's always "almost done" — consider this the nudge. The product isn't the code. The product is what happens when someone else uses it.

Time to find out.
